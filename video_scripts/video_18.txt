0:30-1:00 - How AI Capacity Planning Works
Visual: Agent data inputs: Historical metrics (90 days), Business calendar, Deployment schedule, Traffic patterns, Growth trends. Output: Capacity forecast with confidence intervals.
Voiceover: AI capacity planning agents ingest five data streams continuously. Historical metrics: ninety days minimum of CPU, memory, network, storage, and request volume data for every service. Business calendar: known events, marketing campaigns, product launches, and seasonal patterns that affect traffic. Deployment schedule: upcoming releases that might change resource requirements. Traffic patterns: daily, weekly, and monthly cycles plus trend direction. Growth trends: user acquisition rates, data volume growth, and transaction volume trends. The agent generates capacity forecasts with confidence intervals — not single-point estimates but ranges that account for uncertainty. Forecasts update daily and adjust in real time when patterns shift.

1:00-1:30 - Predictive Scaling vs Reactive Scaling
Visual: Two timeline graphs overlaid. Reactive: traffic spike → latency increase → alert → manual scale → recovery (8 min). Predictive: pre-scale → traffic spike → no latency impact (0 min).
Voiceover: The difference between predictive and reactive scaling is the difference between zero downtime and customer-facing latency. Reactive scaling: traffic spike hits, latency increases, alert fires, engineer or autoscaler responds, new capacity provisioned, recovery. Total impact window: four to eight minutes of degraded performance. Predictive scaling: agent detects pattern indicating traffic increase in forty-five minutes, pre-scales capacity, traffic spike arrives, zero latency impact. The agent learned that every Tuesday at two PM, batch processing jobs spike CPU demand. It pre-scales at one forty-five PM. Every month-end, reporting queries increase database load three hundred percent. It pre-scales storage and compute on the twenty-eighth. Predictive scaling eliminates the performance gap that reactive scaling always has.

1:30-2:00 - Right-Sizing Without Risk
Visual: Right-sizing safety framework: Analyze 90-day peak → Set limit at P95 + 20% headroom → Apply change to staging → Monitor 48 hours → Apply to production with rollback trigger.
Voiceover: Right-sizing without risk requires a systematic safety framework. The agent analyzes ninety days of usage data and identifies the ninety-fifth percentile peak — not average, not maximum, but the level exceeded only five percent of the time. It sets new resource limits at P95 plus twenty percent headroom. Changes apply to staging first with forty-eight hour monitoring. Production changes include automatic rollback triggers: if p99 latency increases more than ten percent or error rate increases more than zero point five percent after a right-sizing change, the agent reverts immediately. This framework means the agent can aggressively right-size development and staging environments — where seventy percent of waste lives — while being conservative on production. No human needs to approve a dev environment downsize. Production changes above twenty-five percent require approval.

2:00-2:30 - Demand Forecasting for Growth
Visual: Growth forecast chart showing current capacity, projected demand at 3/6/12 months, recommended capacity additions with timing and cost.
Voiceover: Demand forecasting connects capacity planning to business planning. The agent projects resource requirements at three, six, and twelve month horizons based on growth trends. Current API service handles ten thousand requests per second at sixty percent CPU. User growth is twelve percent monthly. In six months, you'll need one point eight times current capacity. The agent recommends: add two replicas in month three, upgrade instance type in month five, total additional cost four thousand two hundred dollars per month — versus twenty-three thousand if you over-provision now "just in case." Finance teams love this because capacity planning connects to budget forecasting with data instead of guesses. Engineering teams love it because they stop getting surprised by capacity constraints during growth spurts.

2:30-3:00 - Multi-Dimensional Optimization
Visual: Three optimization dimensions: Performance (latency SLO), Cost (budget target), Resilience (redundancy requirements). Agent finding optimal balance point.
Voiceover: Capacity planning isn't just about cost — it's multi-dimensional optimization. Performance dimension: maintain p99 latency below your SLO target. Cost dimension: stay within budget allocation per team and service. Resilience dimension: maintain minimum redundancy for fault tolerance. The AI agent finds the optimal balance point across all three dimensions. Sometimes the cheapest option violates resilience requirements — the agent selects the cheapest option that maintains N plus one redundancy. Sometimes the performance-optimal option exceeds budget — the agent finds the configuration that meets SLO at lowest cost. This multi-dimensional approach prevents the common failure mode of optimizing for cost alone and creating fragile systems, or optimizing for performance alone and wasting budget.
